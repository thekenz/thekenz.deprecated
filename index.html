# 01

The Community Space

Temple Beth Israel (TBI) approached us with a problem ‚Äî not enough people in the community knew about them enough to rent rooms from them.

We countered with an idea ‚Äî we would create a community of passionate people around the space itself. But we would need to work side by side to make this happen.


The Idea

We wanted to create a separate "not-for-profit" organization -- called "The Community Space" -- inside Temple Beth Israel. This organization's purpose would be to build a relationship with the community at large, through large events, social media, and outreach.


Building a Culture of Inclusivity

We created a new client-engagement paradigm, called The Partnership Model, that allows the clients to work on a particular project alongside a consultant. Instead of the consultants working on a deliverable the entire semester and presenting it to a client, clients would be taking on some of the work themselves to make the project a success.

We found that this model dramatically increased buy-in from the clients, and made the shift from project-creation to project-deployment almost seamless.


The Logo

We created the Community Space logo to represent friendliness, trust, and inclusivity. The brackets on either side of the logo represent the walls of the space itself, holding together the past, present, and future community that within the walls. The typography is bubbly and playful, but solid, conveying a strong and friendly atmosphere. Finally, the tree is meant to represent both the large oak tree that stands prominently within the compound as well as the idea that a strong, vibrant, and stable community is possible, but only if you water it.


Outcome

By April of 2015, The Community Space began officially sponsoring and creating events at the venue. By the end of the 2014-2015 academic year, groundwork was laid for future events sponsored by the Community Space, such as an antique car show, movie nights, and a recurring Food Truck festival. # 02

CWPD Internal Restructuring

In order to transform the CWPD into an empowering community for passionate writers, I reorganized the Center so that our consultants were empowered to make that change themselves.


Team Structure

In order to create this movement of creatives on campus, I needed to empower the consultants by leveraging their skill sets they were cultivating outside of the Center. The I designed a team structure that not only reflected the diversity of interests and skill sets at the Center, but also ultimately addressed key issues for the Center itself.

I eventually settled on four distinct teams:




Metrics

Opening up pipelines to collect data, generating insights



Innovation

Creating new strategic initiatives, creative problem solvers



Social

Partnership development, outreach, consultant development



Content

Marketing and promotion, curating content for CWPD media publications

Implementation

Rather than overseeing a standing committee, I organized the structure so that team leaders operated as individual project managers, overseeing many groups of consultants working on a many of distinct projects for that team.

At the beginning of the year, team leaders would pitch a number of projects to the entire staff. Consultants would then volunteer to commit to work on as many projects as they wanted. If a project did not meet the threshold, it would be killed on for that semester.


Outcome

After one full year of challenges and iterations, the team structure was firmly cemented in the foundation of the CWPD, and ready to be expanded and further molded by a new leader. In this year, despite multiple iterations and revision to the team structure, we as a Center were able to kick off an incredible amount of projects (shown below).

SOURCE Pathways Digital Strategy

I created Pathways as a way for SOURCE to not only educate and inform its extensive network of donors, alumni, and nonprofits, but also to finally generate data to drive key decisions on content and strategy.

Pathways Design

We wanted the design for the SOURCE Pathways Newsletter to be something that delivers content and updates to the nonprofits in the SOURCE Network as quickly as possible. I designed a pared down template with bold headings that allows the email to be skimmed easily.



Using Data to Enrich and Grow Pathways

MailChimp‚Äôs analytics capabilities were a great out-of-the-box way to begin quantifying and understanding client relationships with SOURCE. I lead the effort to begin using methods such as A/B testing to understand how different types of content inspire different methods of action. # 04

Made to Stick Workshops

This was not a workshop about bcoming better at public speaking or about making a "good" presentation. These workshops focused on designing a presentation so that its able to easily retained and recalled by your audience.

Workshop Outline

The biggest challenge of these workshops is convincing an audience of already great public speakers that speaking skills are not enough to get your point across. The best way to do this is to highlight common presentation and content creation habits and create a discussion around why these habits are so useful.

One particularly "sticky" piece of information from the presentation was pretending to go into an extremely boring presentation about myself, only to turn the presentation itself into a conversation about how little information is retained in this format and most importantly, how this format is used almost universally.



Implementation

Prior to the workshop, I ask the participants to bring a piece of writing they‚Äôre working on for a client RIGHT NOW. This could be a piece of advertising copy for a organization, a pitch presentation, or a cover letter. The relevancy is key because if we did this on material that didn't have a direct impact on the individual, there would be less willingness to really try out the concepts covered in the workshop.


Final Thoughts

Since presenting this workshop to SOURCE, The SUCCESs model has become the cornerstone of SOURCE's philosophy on creating effective content. I've since been invited back to give this workshop again to the incoming class of associate consultants to SOURCE. The workshop itself is also consistently evolving, and continues to grow with my own experience and domain knowledge.

# 05

OpenProcure


The High Level

OpenProcure is an application that crowdsources information to create transparency for startups interested in selling their services to government entities. Currently the website is focusing on securing information on procurement.


What is Procurement?

Every city in the United States buys things from private entities. These things can be small, like pens and papers for government offices, or large, like contracting services to make a bridge or a highway. This service of buying things is known as procurement.

Each of these cities has slightly different rules that determines how it can purchase these goods. One of these rules is the procurement threshold, which is a dollar value brightline that, when broken, triggers a complex bidding process for services.

For example, if the procurement threshold is $50,000, any goods or services that cost less than $50,000 require few regulations for purchasing. However, for goods priced above $50,000 that the city wants to buy, the rules for purchasing these goods become very complex. This effectively creates a barrier to entry for small startups who have neither the expertise nor capital to sell their services to government entities.


Why Does Procurement Matter?

Illogical municipal procurement regulations may be the single biggest reason for why government software is badly designed, outdated, and extremely expensive. Its one of the reasons why Healthcare.gov failed so loudly. It wastes millions of dollars of taxpayer money every year, deploying well outdated software services that make our government run slower.

More on procurement below:

- Procurement 101 (Code for America)
- Procurement and Civic Innovation (DataMade)
- Fixing Procurement (Department of Better Technology)



How Does OpenProcure Help?

Procurement thresholds are notoriously buried in municipal codes. This lack of visibility is a huge barrier to entry for startups, who aren't familiar enough with procurement or procurement thresholds to know where to look. I spent some time parsing through municipal codes for some of the largest cities and counties in the US to build up an initial dataset, and then worked with the founders of MuniRent (a civic tech startup) host the data online and create a process for updating the data. This is not enough to get startups to begin selling to government, but its a step in the right direction.

# 06

"Spirits and Animals" CMC Tank Design


Summary

ASCMC held a competition for a commemorative design for apparel for the kickoff event for the 2014 - 2015 school year at CMC. This design won the competition, and was printed on over 500 pieces of apparel for the event.


Design Intent for the Front of Shirt

The theme of the event was "Spirits and Animals". There was a certain duality about these two features that I found very interesting. "Spirits", of course refers to the ethereal, the otherworldly, and the mysterious. "Animals" refers to things that are earthly, raw, and primal. for "Spirits", I wanted to use a font that was equal parts delicate and strong. "Animals" was a slightly easier choice -- I wanted a decorative font that was a little dirtier, and rougher, almost like someone was paiting the letters in mud. Finally, I wrapped the type in the characteristic "Stag" Antlers, to illustrate that the CMC Stag is the Spirit Animal that we were all channeling for the first event of the year.


Design Intent for the Back of Shirt

The party itself is called "6:01", because it famous commences exactly at 6:01pm on the first Saturday of the year. I liked the idea of using various animals -- one graceful (Zebra), one racuous (Gorilla), and one that's a blend of the two (Lion) -- to paint the numbers of the event on the back of the shirt. Finally, the message ends with a call to action -- "Find yours", prompting students to use this event as the beginning of a journey to find their inner "Spirit Animal" during the year.


  • Client: ASCMC
  • Tags: Design
  • Timeline: August - September 2014
