# 01

The Community Space


Objective

Temple Beth Israel (TBI) approached us with a simple objective: get more people interested and excited about renting rooms from the organization. They had previously only been marketing to their congregation and wanted to branch out and get more publicity from the greater Inland Empire Community.


Analysis

After a brief competitive analysis, we realized that there were two types of rental spaces -- extremely expensive venues meant for weddings and corporate events, and rental spaces from religious institutions. Both were equally divisive. Given that TBI was a religious institution itself, we needed to break the mold of what a rental space could be, and started thinking about the notion of inclusiveness as a driving force for this campaign.


Building a Culture of Inclusivity

In order for us to create a truly inclusive brand, we needed the process that creates the brand to be as inclusive as well. To accomplish this, we created an entirely new collaboration paradigm within SOURCE, which we dubbed "The Partnership Model" (in contrast to the regular client-stakeholder model). Instead of simply working for the client and creating deliverables, everyone involved (both clients and stakeholders) would work on different parts of the project. This not only baked rapid iteration and feedback into our process, it also ensured that engagement among the stakeholders would remain high.


Outcome

Because of our focus on inclusion as a brand, we began to start crafting events that brought people together. The Community Space was selected to be the inagural venue for the kickoff event for SOURCE's Brown Bag series, which brought together representatives from over a dozen different nonprofit organizations. The Community Space also began creating and planning events that were targeted at the entire community, such as an antique car show in June 2015, and a Food Truck emporium event in October 2015. Our team also left behind a solid foundation and client relationship for the next SOURCE team.


The Logo

The Community Space logo cemented the idea of inclusivity within the organization. The typography is bubbly and playful, but solid, and bold, conveying a strong, but friendly atmosphere. The brackets on either side of the logo represent the walls of the center itself, housing and holding together past, present, and future community that already resides within the walls. Finally, the tree represents the large oak tree that stands prominently within the compound, providing shade and shelther. It also represents the growth of a network from one focal point, signifying that a strong, vibrant, and stable community is possible, but only if you water it.

  • Client: SOURCE / Temple Beth Israel
  • Tags: Design, Strategy
  • Timeline: September 2014 - May 2015

# 02

CWPD Internal Restructuring


Objective

We wanted to transform The Center for Writing and Public Discourse (CWPD) into a creative community for students. In order to live up to that vision, we needed to make bold moves by mobilizing the creative power of the consultants themselves.


Team Structure

In order to create this movement of creatives on campus, we needed to empower the consultants of the Writing Center from the bottom up, instead of from the top down. We therefore needed a team structure that reflected the diversity of interests and skill sets at the Center, but also ultimately addressed key issues for the Center itself.

We eventually settled on four distinct teams:




Metrics

Opening up pipelines to collect data, and generateing insights



Innovation

CWPD’s “Idealab”, where bold new strategic projects would be created



Social

Partnership development, outreach, and consultant development



Content

Marketing and promotion, and curating content for CWPD media publications

Implementation

Initially these teams resembled standing committees that required every consultant to commit to one team for the entire semester. Very quickly, I realized that this was too restrictive on the consultants, so I shifted the team structure to a project based model. Instead of leading one group of consultants to tackle many projects, the team leaders operated as individual project managers, overseeing many groups of consultants working on a many of distinct projects for that team.

Team leaders would pitch a number of projects at the beginning of the year to the entire staff. Consultants would then voluntarily commit to as many projects as they wanted. If a project did not meet the threshold, it would not be worked on for that semester.


Outcome

After one full year of challenges and iterations, the team structure was firmly cemented in the foundation of the CWPD, and ready to be expanded and further molded by a new leader.


Projects

— Write-ins (Social)
— Student Slam Poetry Workshops and Open Mics (Social)
— Discourse Magazine, a CWPD independent and student sourced publication (Content)
— Moved data collection and reporting from consultation sessions from TutorTrac to Qualtrics (Metrics)
— Remote Writing Center Consultations (Innovation)

  • Client: The Center for Writing and Public Discourse
  • Tags: Strategy
  • Timeline: September 2014 - May 2015
